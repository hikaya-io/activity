PROGRAM HUBS

The path of a refugee…

According to UNHCR, more than 1 million migrants and refugees crossed into Europe in 2015 - the vast majority of which traveled along the Aegean Route from countries such as Syria, Afghanistan, and Iraq through Turkey, Greece and the Balkans. Mercy Corps is strategically positioned with teams in active areas along the Aegean Migration Route.

WHO IS MOVING THROUGH THE AEGEAN?

Over 4 million Syrian refugees. 2.6 million displaced Iraqis. Conflict is tearing countries apart. People are moving at a greater rate than ever before. Who are the primary populations moving? The simple answer is: everyone. Routes are now flooded by families fleeing homes and countries that are no longer safe or habitable.

Where are people coming from? Who is staying in Turkey versus moving on to Greece and the Balkans? RRIMA Report 1 focuses on who Mercy Corps is serving along the Aegean Migration Route.

FAMILY PROFILES

In the process of analyzing data for the RRIMA subject reports, the team leveraged collected data and its findings to create sample representative households for 4 of the locations it has reported on in Turkey and Greece. In presenting these profiles, the team seeks to provide an example of the size and makeup of a household and to highlight a few of the typical challenges that household members may face. While representative household profiles are intended to be an average example of a household from region to region, a wide range of households and related challenges was seen across all locations. For issue-specific findings, please see the RRIMA Reports directly.

The average refugee household size in Turkey was larger than in Greece. Single travelers and young couples traveling without families were more likely to travel onward to Greece than families with children. The majority of refugees in Turkey are Syrian, while refugees in Greece come from many different countries of origin (Syria, Afghanistan, Iraq, Iran, and Pakistan).

WHAT CHALLENGES ARE THEY FACING?

Refugees, asylum seekers and migrant populations each have their own story for why and how they left their country of origin. But all are arriving in a foreign country with limited resources and knowledge of the language, government policies and services available to them.

Turkey is host to at least 2.75 million Syrian refugees (among others). What are the primary needs and concerns for these populations? What issues are they running into? RRIMA Report 2 focuses on needs and challenges for refugee populations in Izmir, Turkey.

WHERE IS EVERYONE GOING?

Y2016 has been a tumultuous year for migration patterns across the Mediterranean and European routes.This massive movement of people has placed a strain on existing resources and support services that has never been experienced before.

Which countries have received the most arrivals, and how has that changed over time? How do challenges and needs for refugees change along the route? RRIMA Report 3 looks at a timeline of events since January 2016, mapping out movements and highlighting moments of note this year.

WHAT COPING MECHANSIMS ARE BEING USED?

In both Turkey and Syria there are many barriers to working and earning enough income to support a family. Despite the fact that there are support services in place, that is often not enough to meet the needs of those who have been displaced by conflict.

How are displaced persons, both within and outside of Syria, coping with the challenges they are facing? Are they able to get work? If so, are they earning enough to support their families? RRIMA Report #4 explores the coping mechanisms most in use in North Syria and across the Turkish border, in Gaziantep.

RRIMA Report Vol. 4

PROGRAM END-OF-YEAR REFLECTIONS

Both the PRM-funded MALUMAT program in Gaziantep, Turkey and the ECHO-funded Aegean Response program in Izmir, Turkey are concluding and transitioning into their next phase(s). This report serves to wrap up the analysis for the current phase of programming and to provide some reflections and comparisons not provided in previous reports.

What do livelihoods and coping strategies look like in Izmir in comparison to Gaziantep? What are the push and pull factors in Gaziantep? Do those differ from the push and pull factors identified for Izmir? RRIMA Report #5 explores these questions, as well as provides some final reflections about program operations.
